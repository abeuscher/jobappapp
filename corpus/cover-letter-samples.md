# Cover letter samples

Five real cover letters, kept verbatim. **Primary purpose: voice and framing.**
These show how Alex actually writes about himself — direct, self-aware,
business-problem-first, no inflated enthusiasm.

> **Note to the generation layer:** these letters are several years old.
> Time-anchored claims in them ("in my current role…", "over 20 years…",
> team sizes, side clients) are historical — do NOT restate them as current.
> For current facts use `facts.md` and `experience-bank.md`. Reuse the voice,
> the framing moves, and the historical anecdotes (clearly framed as past).

## Blizzard — Director of Engineering, Web Applications

To Whom It May Concern,

I am writing to apply for the position of Director of Engineering, Web Applications. I have been working on the web for over 20 years, and been passionate about gaming for about 35 plus. I have worked for companies in a variety of industries but the role in which I believe I had the greatest impact was at 2K Games where I spent several years helping to overhaul their web solutions for their titles.

The area of this job in which I particularly excel is in stakeholder management and architecture. I am never going to write the most optimal code in existence; I am not an innovator in that area and it is not where my strengths lie. I can explain closures and pass a white board test, but it just isn't what I am best at. Where I excel is in sitting down with people, understanding their business problems, and solving them with websites. This is not strictly a technical or non-technical process, and involves awareness of politics, finance, and common sense.

I like to think of myself as a "well rounded" developer, in the sense that I have switched industries often enough to understand what all web projects have in common versus which problems are industry specific. Ages ago when I worked in rock radio I was tasked with increasing our online engagement. This was almost entirely a non-technical problem and involved acquiring video rights to our in studio performances, coaching our on-air talent on how to write blogs, and giving training sessions on engaging with listeners over social media. Conversely, at 2K Games, the task was to bring our web development in-house. This required an entirely technical solution which involved moving the team to a headless CMS paradigm and working with the developer of the product to adapt it to our needs. We then built an end to end continuous deployment system with our DevOps team and the system is now actually integrated with 2K's games as well as their websites.

I can begin to guess what the challenges of this role are – disparate stakeholders, competing priorities, and a large quantity of moving deadlines. I am well equipped and have deep experience in bringing situations like this from chaos into order and I hope we can have the opportunity to discuss how I might approach this set of challenges within your organization.

Thanks for your time,
Alex Beuscher

## Recurring moves worth reusing (voice guide)

- Opens plainly: "I am writing to apply for the position of X." No throat-clearing.
- Leads with candid self-assessment, including what he's *not* best at (Blizzard letter) — credibility through honesty.
- Frames strength as: sitting down with people, understanding business problems, solving them with websites; stakeholder management and architecture over code golf.
- Uses one concrete past anecdote matched to the employer's likely problem ("I have solved the problem you have before").
- Names the employer's probable pain points and offers to discuss an approach — "from chaos into order."
- Mentions genuine personal connection to the company's domain when it exists (gaming, cannabis, Patreon creators) — and never fakes one.
- Signs off briefly: "Thanks for your time."
