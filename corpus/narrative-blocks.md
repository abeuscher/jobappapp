## Why founder-operator

I've spent 25 years shipping web products inside other people's companies —
radio stations, game studios, SaaS vendors, a drone maker — usually as the
person connecting engineering to the rest of the business, and partnering
directly with the CEO at nearly every stop. The last few years I've applied
that end to end: founding two ventures where I owned product, architecture,
and engineering myself. I'm most useful at small companies where one person
who can plan, build, and talk to stakeholders replaces a lot of coordination
overhead.

## AI / agentic workflows

I build with LLMs as core architecture, not as a bolt-on. I built MAIA, an
open-source medical AI assistant integrating nine LLM engines with
consent-based access to a patient's EHR timeline, which became the reference
implementation for an international hackathon. I'm currently building an
AI-native CRM solo, using agentic-coding workflows to ship at a scope that
normally requires a full team, and I train other practitioners on integrating
LLM tooling into their work safely and effectively.

## Leading distributed teams

I've led distributed teams in several configurations: a remote team in Mumbai
at Contentstack (project management, code review, mentorship, cross-timezone
coordination), and a mixed team of employees, a designer, and third-party
contractors at Skydio (sprint planning, code reviews, stakeholder
communications). I'm comfortable being the person accountable for both the
code and the communication around it.

## Writing and communication

My degree is in Writing, Literature & Publishing, and my first career was
content — running websites and producing video for Boston radio stations.
That background shows up in how I work as an engineer: documentation,
stakeholder communication, and training are things I do well and actually
enjoy.
